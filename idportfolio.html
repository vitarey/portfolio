​Outgoing and sociable, Vita Rey discovered a passion for Interior Design as the field engaged both her love of people and her love of artistic expression. She wanted to have a large scale impact on how people interpret their environment by designing the very spaces that people exist in. Sustainability and accessibility was the focus of Vita’s education at the Interior Design program at San Francisco State University. When she graduated in 2014, she began her career at Restaurant Design Concepts and quickly rose to the position of Principal Project Manager and Senior Designer. Entrusted with this responsibility, she leads the firm’s projects to fruition for all new restaurants in the Bay Area, while focusing on accessibility and including as many sustainable choices as possible.

Elmore's American Tavern
Fremont, CA

Elmore’s American Tavern is a two-story eatery commissioned by a Fremont native with a hope to invigorate the historic Niles District. The client wanted to include elements of the building’s historical past on the exterior while showcasing a chic, French-style bistro interior with bars and patios on each floor. Her vision was to have a fully-accessible restaurant with an elevator connecting both levels. The client’s ideal patrons would be locals who would be thrilled to finally have a place to eat, chat, and relax.

Location

​A key lot that our client was watching finally went up for sale and she leapt at the opportunity to transform it into a much needed restaurant and bar. She worked with the planning department to allow her to expand the building and add a second story in order to make the best use of the triangular 2,100 sqft lot. The proposed building’s exterior board and batten design would pay homage to the original gas station that once stood there in the 1920’s. 

Concept

This client wanted the interior of her restaurant to evoke a crisp upscale feel with plenty of color and texture throughout the building. We incorporated as much daylight as possible by adding sliding glass NanaWalls that seamlessly expanded the dining areas into their respective patios. The health department required that the cooking areas be enclosed so we specified a custom sealed glass partition to create the illusion of one long, unbroken bar counter that the client desired. The custom ceilings used reclaimed wood from the original building to create a three-dimensional look on both floors. We specified finishes made from recycled material as a priority throughout the design process.

Aburaya and Garden House
Oakland, CA

​Aburaya and Garden House are two different eateries, Japanese style fried chicken and a build-your-own salad bar, that belong to a brother and sister. They shared the original space and kitchen in a single location, alternating between daytime salads for the local businesses and evening fried chicken for the bar hoppers. These clients approached us to redesign their restaurant to fit both visions within the shared space. This was a fun challenge to accommodate the two different styles of service in a narrow building, while still maintaining accessible clearances behind the counters and in the public right of way. They both share a love of punk rock and chickens, and wanted to celebrate their Oakland roots.

Location

​This renovation was a big upgrade for their long-time small business in downtown Oakland. Located two blocks from BART, this long and narrow space was a local favorite that had customers coming in all day for custom salads and all night for Japanese fried chicken. The existing space had one small kitchen that was too tight to have both concepts running at the same time and they desperately needed to spread out.

Concept

​These two clients had very different concepts for their style of food service. Garden House required a line-up space for guests to look at the diverse salad toppings options and order a custom salad specific to their desires.  Conversely, Aburaya aimed to serve the local nightlife and presented a very simple menu meant to crank out delicious meals for the masses. In the new design, the siblings wanted their respective kitchens on opposite ends and a small dining area in the center. We pored over the building codes to grapple with the clearance allowances over the entire length of the building, with the emphasis on making sure customers would be comfortable entering and leaving the building. We chose to specify reclaimed wood in the design to give Aburaya’s Japanese inspired elements a grungy, punk-rock twist.

Pho
San Jose, CA

​Pho San Jose was a project in which four friends wished to open a restaurant in their home town of San Jose. They each had their own expertise to bring to the table, two chefs, a businessman, and a restaurant manager who also was a gardening enthusiast. They wanted to open a place to showcase their excellent Vietnamese cooking and to stand out from the average Pho restaurant. They wanted a warm, airy space with lots of natural light so that the plant lover could grow his own green wall and living decorations. These specifications dovetailed well with sustainable design elements such as daylighting to lower the overall energy footprint.

Location

​As San Jose continues to grow as a tech hub, with many office parks opening up all over the city, the demand to feed all the workers local, delicious meals continues to grow as well. But it is not enough to merely offer excellent meals, the space must be beautiful as well. Located by the 680 freeway entrance and the Berryessa light rail stop, this location serves as a great spot to meet for lunch or dinner.

Concept

​The most critical request was to bring in more sunlight and to emphasize natural elements. Four new hipped skylights expanded beyond the original ceiling and made the dining area feel more spacious. Above the patrons, a cluster of reclaimed wood frames with trailing plants filled the center of the open, airy space.  A green plant wall was prominently mounted against the textured plaster that adorned the high walls. Recycled thin brick covered the entrance wall and we refinished the existing concrete floor. Wooden elements warmed the space throughout and made for an intimate settee eating area. Health requirements necessitated a dropped ceiling over the service area, so we hung a glass ceiling framed with square black metal mullions to utilize as much of the natural light as possible.
